Sign language, any means of communication through bodily movements, especially of the hands and arms, used when spoken communication is impossible or not desirable. The practice is probably older than speech. Sign language may be as coarsely expressed as mere grimaces, shrugs, or pointings; or it may employ a delicately nuanced combination of coded manual signals reinforced by facial expression and perhaps augmented by words spelled out in a manual alphabet. Wherever vocal communication is impossible, as between speakers of mutually unintelligible languages or when one or more would-be communicators is deaf, sign language can be used to bridge the gap.

Sign language is a visual means of communication that uses hand gestures, facial expressions, and body movements to convey meaning. Here are some key uses and aspects of sign language:
  1. Communication for the Deaf and Hard of Hearing: Sign language is primarily used by individuals who are deaf or hard of hearing as their primary mode of communication. It allows them to express thoughts, ideas, and emotions effectively.
  2. Accessibility: It serves as a vital tool for accessibility, enabling communication between deaf individuals, and between deaf and hearing individuals who have learned sign language.
  3. Cultural Identity: Sign languages often have regional variations and are integral to deaf culture. They provide a sense of identity and community among deaf people.
  4. Education: Sign language is used in educational settings for deaf students, facilitating learning and social interaction.
  5. Legal and Official Recognition: In many countries, sign languages are recognized as official languages, ensuring access to services and rights for deaf individuals.
  6. Interpreter Services: Professional sign language interpreters facilitate communication between deaf and hearing individuals in various settings such as conferences, medical appointments, and legal proceedings.
  7. Artistic Expression: Sign language can be expressive and artistic, used in performances such as storytelling, poetry, and theater.
  8. Emergency Situations: In emergencies where verbal communication is hindered (e.g., loud environments or when silence is required), sign language can be crucial for effective communication.
